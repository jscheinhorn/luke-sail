Seasickness?

Stugeron is sold on the island. It is like Dramamine. We used it every day we sailed last time and were fine.

Food?

Provisioning is possible ahead of time through 12 Knots charter broker or Paradise Foods

Each boat can determine which option suits them best and how meal tasks will be assigned throughout the week or if breakfasts and lunches will be done on an individual basis.

Currency

Eastern Caribbean dollar is fixed at 2.67 EC to 1 USD. US dollars are widely accepted. Visa and Mastercard are widely accepted.

Is it possible to sail on another boat?

It is possible to rotate during sails as long as each boat has enough experienced crew for their skipper to sail. Please check with your skipper(s).

Why a catamaran?

They have two hulls and don't roll as much as a monohull. Comfort and spaciousness.

What's a dinghy?

We ride the dinghy to and from the shore.

It's a smallish, semi-rigid boat with an outboard motor that we put into the water to get the rest of the way to land when the catamaran is moored / at anchor.

Will I have internet?

Most of the time.

Can buy a prepaid SIM card for $25-50 EC to put in your phone if you don’t have a global plan. There is lots of WiFi on islands, including at bars and restaurants. Good to use WhatsApp for WiFi calling and messaging.

Local etiquette / expectations

Clothing / Nudity

Away from the beach and at any town, bar, restaurant, etc. etiquette is to wear a shirt and shorts/skirt, etc., not beachwear. Toplessness is illegal on most Islands.

Tipping

10% is standard if a service charge is not already included in the bill. It’s not always expected.

Taxis

Set the price before getting in. Make sure you’re talking the same currency, EC or US

Drugs

Marijuana legal on St Vincent - thanks to the rastas! medical licenses available at coconut grove beach club in blue lagoon and at plantation house in bequia. Keep it to less than an ounce for good measure. Other drug laws are very strict and include imprisonment.

What safety equipment is there?

Life raft, EPIRB, VHF Radios, life vests, flares. We will have a safety briefing on the first day to go over the safety equipment on the boat and any protocols, including day-to-day practices for safe use of the gas stoves, closing hatches, etc.

Bugs and natural dangers?

Stripy legged mosquitoes can carry disease although being on the water you’re much less likely to encounter them. They breed more in rainy months. Bug spray and mosquito coils can help keep them and other bugs at bay, especially around sunset.

Scorpions and centipedes are poisonous but not deadly and are rare.

Manchineel trees have toxic yellow-green apples and its leaves can give you a poison ivy-like rash. They’re good to stand under for shade, but not in the rain. On beaches you’ll sometimes find them identified with signs. Fruit and leaves Tree

Sea urchins can hurt your feet in the water.

Be aware of boats in the area when swimming and snorkeling.

No known shark attacks are on record in the Windward Islands. You might see a nurse shark. These are generally harmless unless provoked.

Don’t touch coral.

Sea ants can cause some tingling and jellyfish can sting. A little vinegar soak helps for stings.

Do I need a certification to scuba dive?

Many dive shops will give you a short PADI primer course to train you and will guide you to limited depths (~40ft) if you do not have a certification but still want to dive

Do I need to know how to sail? Will I sail the boat?

The boats are arranged so that there is a sufficient number of trained skippers, experienced crew, and crew on each boat. Skippers have either RYA Day Skipper or ASA Bareboat certification or equivalent experience. There are plenty of sailing duties to help with for willing and able crew, including hoistng and dousing sails, dropping anchor, picking up moorings, being a lookout. These skills can be conferred to you by your skippers/experienced crew. Let your skipper know if you'd like to have a hand on the helm!

What are the basic boat terms I will hear on board?

Pictures and video coming soon!

  • Fore / Forward - anything forward of the mast
  • Aft - anything behind the mast
  • Bow - the very front of the boat
  • Stern - the very back of the boat
  • Port - the left side of the boat if facing the bow
  • Starboard - the right side of the boat if facing the bow
  • Tack - turning the boat so that the origin of the wind comes at us from the bow
  • Gybe / Jibe - turning the boat so that the origin of the wind comes at us from the stern
  • Main sail
  • Jib sail - smaller (generally), forward sail
  • Boom - don't let it hit you!
  • Parts of the sail
    • Tack
    • Clew
    • Head
    • Luff
    • Leach
    • Foot
  • Winch
  • Cabin
  • Cockpit
  • Deck
  • Windward - the side closer to the origin of the wind (used in relation to something downwind)
  • Leeward - the downwind side (used in relation to something that is upwind)
  • Knots - nautical miles per hour, 1.15 miles per hour
